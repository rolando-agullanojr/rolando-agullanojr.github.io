Mobile devices have been an integral part of the IT industry and will continue to iterate. As society continues to streamline and be more mobile, so does the concept of a computer device. Originally, computing systems were seen in large mainframe systems that would complete simple calculations or tasks. An example of this is the IBM 705 mainframe computer that was created in the mid 1950s. This was the first mainframe to be built, but required a large space, equivalent to an entire room (Ebbers, 2011). In recent years, we now have equivalent computing power seen in mobile devices that can be held within the palm of our hands. This progression of a computer incorporates all functionality that were originally separate, known as device convergence. The concept of device convergence was that you could combine a telephone, a music player, GPS, etc., into a compact mobile device that can be transported anywhere. No longer did devices need to be separated by functionality; thus, adding more convenience in an age of efficiency. Mobiles devices such as laptops, smartphones, tablets, smart watches, all demonstrate device convergence with continuous iterations being developed to this day. The main advantages of having mobile devices is ease of access. If I needed to search a restaurant location or call my co-worker to inform them of the location, I did not need a large-scale computer in order to complete all of these tasks. With these concepts of adding efficiency, it also contains drawbacks. Mobile devices allow for an easy access of human interaction over the Internet, but this is a two-sided dagger. Mobile devices have increased the amount of disconnect at a social level. People tend to look at their phones or focus on their devices instead of connecting verbally or physically, all due to the more efficient mobile usage. This social disconnect is a negative aspect of having mobile devices, but will remain an integral part of the IT industry now and for the future.
